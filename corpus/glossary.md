# Financial Glossary (seed corpus)

## Asset allocation
The mix of stocks, bonds, cash, and alternatives in a portfolio. A common starting heuristic for the equity share is `110 - age`, adjusted up for higher risk tolerance and down for lower. Allocation is the single biggest driver of long-term return variance.

## Bond
A debt instrument: the issuer (government, corporation) borrows money from the buyer and pays periodic interest (coupon) until maturity, when the face value is repaid. Bond prices move inversely to interest rates.

## Compound interest
Interest paid on both the original principal and on accumulated interest. The future value of a lump sum invested at rate `r` for `n` periods is `FV = PV * (1 + r)^n`. Compounding is what makes early retirement contributions disproportionately valuable.

## Diversification
Spreading investments across uncorrelated assets to reduce idiosyncratic risk. Diversification cannot eliminate market (systematic) risk, but it can dramatically reduce single-stock or single-sector risk.

## Dollar-cost averaging (DCA)
Investing a fixed dollar amount on a regular schedule, regardless of price. Smooths entry timing and removes emotion, but typically underperforms lump-sum investing in rising markets because money sits in cash longer.

## ETF (Exchange-Traded Fund)
A pooled investment vehicle traded on an exchange like a stock. Most ETFs track an index. Compared to mutual funds, ETFs are usually cheaper, more tax-efficient, and intraday-tradable.

## Expense ratio
The annual fee charged by a fund, expressed as a percentage of assets. A 0.10% expense ratio costs $10/year per $10,000 invested. Over 30 years a 1% gap in expense ratios can cut final wealth by 25%+ due to compounding drag.

## 401(k)
A US employer-sponsored, tax-advantaged retirement account. Traditional 401(k) contributions are pre-tax (taxed on withdrawal); Roth 401(k) contributions are post-tax (tax-free withdrawals on qualified distributions). 2025 contribution limit is $23,500 under age 50.

## IRA (Individual Retirement Account)
A US tax-advantaged retirement account opened by an individual.
- **Traditional IRA**: contributions may be tax-deductible; withdrawals taxed in retirement; Required Minimum Distributions (RMDs) start at age 73.
- **Roth IRA**: contributions are post-tax; qualified withdrawals (after age 59½ and 5-year rule) are tax-free; no RMDs for the original owner; income phase-outs apply.

## Index fund
A mutual fund or ETF designed to mirror a market index (e.g., S&P 500). Low cost and broad diversification make index funds the default recommendation for most long-horizon investors.

## P/E ratio (Price-to-Earnings)
Stock price divided by earnings per share. A higher P/E suggests the market expects faster earnings growth (or that the stock is overvalued). Compare P/Es within a sector, not across.

## Risk tolerance
The level of return volatility an investor can emotionally and financially withstand without abandoning their plan. Often categorized low / moderate / high, and informs asset allocation. True risk tolerance is revealed in drawdowns, not surveys.

## RSI (Relative Strength Index)
A momentum oscillator (0–100) that measures the speed and change of price movements. Conventional thresholds: above 70 is "overbought", below 30 is "oversold". RSI is one of many signals; it is not predictive on its own.

## Sector
A grouping of companies in similar industries (Technology, Healthcare, Financials, Energy, etc.). Sector tilts in a portfolio drive a meaningful share of return differences vs. a broad index.

## Stock (equity)
A share of ownership in a corporation. Stockholders have a residual claim on profits (often paid as dividends) and assets after creditors and bondholders.

## Sharpe ratio
Excess return per unit of volatility, `(R - Rf) / σ`. Higher is better. Useful for comparing risk-adjusted performance, but assumes returns are normally distributed (often violated in practice).

## Volatility
The standard deviation of returns over a period. High volatility ≠ high risk of permanent loss; equities have high volatility but historically positive long-term drift.

## Tax-loss harvesting
Selling a losing position to realize a capital loss that offsets capital gains (or up to $3,000/year of ordinary income). Watch the wash-sale rule: rebuying a "substantially identical" security within 30 days disallows the loss.

## Yield curve
The plot of interest rates against maturity for bonds of equivalent credit quality. A normal curve slopes upward; an inverted curve (short-term rates above long-term) has historically preceded US recessions.

## Disclaimer
The content above is general educational information, not personalized investment advice. Tax rules change; verify current limits and rules with the IRS or a licensed advisor before acting.
